## Executive Summary

If we want to enter this market credibly, we do **not** need to match every competitor feature.

But we do need to cover the core category promise:

- realistic practice
- clear feedback
- repeatable improvement
- team visibility

The market does not reward "AI chat" by itself.

It rewards:

- scenario-based rehearsal
- scoring
- coaching
- readiness visibility

The shortest useful framing is:

- `scenario + persona + simulation + scoring + manager view`

That is the real minimum viable market entry stack.

## Critical Features: Must Have

These are the features we likely need just to be taken seriously in the market.

### 1. Scenario-Based Roleplay

The product must support structured scenarios, not just open chat.

Required capabilities:

- choose a scenario
- define the learner role
- define the AI counterpart role
- define goals or expected outcome
- set difficulty or tone

Why this is critical:

- every serious competitor is selling practice inside realistic use cases
- buyers are paying for rehearsal of specific situations, not for generic conversation

### 2. Custom Persona / Role Configuration

We need AI counterparts that feel role-specific.

Required capabilities:

- persona name and role
- tone / demeanor
- level of pushback
- domain context
- goal or bias

Why this is critical:

- realism depends more on role fidelity than on generic fluency
- competitors repeatedly emphasize custom personas, avatars, buyer profiles, or virtual actors

### 3. Real-Time Conversation Simulation

The user must be able to actually practice the conversation, not just read advice.

Required capabilities:

- live interaction
- low-latency turn-taking
- realistic challenge or pushback
- voice and/or text mode

Minimum recommendation:

- start with `voice-first` or `voice + text`

Why this is critical:

- practice must feel live and responsive
- strong competitors increasingly push toward real-time roleplay rather than delayed simulation

### 4. Feedback After the Session

This is non-negotiable.

Required capabilities:

- overall score
- strengths
- weaknesses
- concrete suggestions
- transcript or replay

Why this is critical:

- without feedback, the product is just an AI conversation toy
- with feedback, it becomes a training product

### 5. Rubric-Based Evaluation

The system needs explicit scoring criteria.

Required capabilities:

- configurable rubric dimensions
- scenario-level evaluation criteria
- scored output by dimension

Typical dimensions:

- clarity
- empathy
- objection handling
- structure
- tone
- confidence

Why this is critical:

- every serious B2B product is moving toward measurement, not just realism
- buyers want consistency, not vibes

### 6. Repetition and Replay

The learner needs to improve through multiple attempts.

Required capabilities:

- re-run scenario
- compare attempts
- optionally retry hard moments

Why this is critical:

- the real value of these products is repeated practice
- one-shot simulations are much weaker commercially

### 7. Admin / Manager Visibility

If we want B2B demand, this is essential.

Required capabilities:

- learner progress dashboard
- scenario completion tracking
- score history
- team view

Why this is critical:

- managers and enablement teams need visibility
- competitors repeatedly emphasize dashboards, reporting, and readiness views

## Strongly Recommended Features

These are not the absolute minimum, but they are quickly becoming table stakes in the stronger part of the market.

### 8. Scenario Authoring for Customers

Customers should be able to create or customize their own scenarios.

Recommended capabilities:

- scenario builder
- persona builder
- rubric editor
- custom prompts / instructions

Why it matters:

- static libraries are not enough for enterprise adoption
- customers want their own conversations, not generic examples

### 9. Content Ingestion

This is becoming a major practical advantage.

Recommended capabilities:

- upload playbooks
- upload call transcripts
- upload training docs
- ingest URLs, decks, manuals, or knowledge docs

Why it matters:

- this helps the simulation sound company-specific fast
- Second Nature and Simmie especially push this pattern

### 10. Readiness / Certification Workflows

The system should support more than informal practice.

Recommended capabilities:

- pass/fail thresholds
- certification scenarios
- benchmark gates
- manager approval or review

Why it matters:

- this is where training becomes operational infrastructure
- Simmie, Yoodli, and Second Nature all lean in this direction

### 11. Multi-Mode Delivery

Not every use case wants the same interface.

Recommended capabilities:

- voice mode
- text mode
- optional avatar mode

Why it matters:

- voice often matters for realism
- text may still be useful for lower-friction practice and certain enterprise environments

### 12. Integrations

Recommended capabilities:

- LMS
- SSO
- CRM
- ATS or HRIS depending on target wedge

Why it matters:

- integrations are often necessary for enterprise rollout
- they reduce friction in adoption and reporting

### 13. Assignment and Team Workflows

Recommended capabilities:

- assign scenarios to learners
- group cohorts
- due dates
- progress by team

Why it matters:

- this is how the product moves from tool to program

## High-Value Differentiators

These features are not required to enter, but they can make the product stand out.

### 14. Communication Delivery Coaching

This is Yoodli’s strongest extra layer.

Examples:

- pacing
- filler words
- tone
- confidence
- verbal clarity

Why it matters:

- many products simulate what to say
- fewer products coach how to say it

### 15. Multilingual Support

This is increasingly important.

Why it matters:

- global teams need realistic practice in operating languages
- this can become a strong wedge in under-served markets

### 16. Multi-Persona / Multi-Agent Scenarios

Examples:

- interview panels
- buying committees
- team conflict discussions

Why it matters:

- this is one of the clearest future moats
- most real conversations are not purely one-on-one

### 17. Analytics Beyond a Basic Score

Examples:

- skill trends over time
- stakeholder coverage
- improvement by rubric dimension
- readiness distribution across a team

Why it matters:

- buyers want operational insight, not just session summaries

### 18. Audit / Compliance Records

This matters if we target regulated industries.

Examples:

- stored transcripts
- evidence of completion
- benchmark records
- policy-aware scoring

Why it matters:

- this can unlock compliance, finance, healthcare, and recruiting use cases

### 19. AR/VR or Immersive Layer

This is not required for entry.

It is only strategically useful when:

- physical context matters
- stress and presence matter
- the environment changes the quality of practice

Why it matters:

- Virti shows this can be powerful
- but it is a differentiator, not a baseline requirement

## Features That Are Nice, But Not Core For Entry

These may help marketing, but they are not what gets us into the market credibly.

### 20. Highly Photorealistic Avatars

Useful, but not essential.

Why:

- most buyers care more about scenario realism and feedback quality than visual polish

### 21. Face Import / Digital Twin Creation

Not a market-entry requirement.

Why:

- currently a niche layer
- adds privacy and implementation complexity
- does not appear to be mainstream B2B table stakes

### 22. Full AR/VR-first Experience

Not necessary unless we intentionally target immersive training segments.

## Recommended MVP

If we want the smallest serious market-entry product, the MVP should include:

1. voice or voice+text roleplay
2. configurable scenarios
3. configurable personas
4. rubric-based scoring
5. feedback report with transcript
6. learner history / repeat practice
7. manager dashboard
8. basic scenario assignment

That would already be enough to position as:

- a credible AI roleplay and readiness platform

## Best First Wedge

The easiest commercial wedge is probably:

- `sales / customer-facing conversation training`

Why:

- the ROI is easier to explain
- repetition is already culturally accepted
- playbooks exist
- managers feel coaching pain immediately

Second-best wedge:

- `leadership / difficult workplace conversations`

Best strategic long-term wedge:

- `multi-agent high-stakes business conversations`

## What We Cannot Skip

If we skip these, we will likely look weak versus competitors:

- real scenarios
- believable personas
- structured feedback
- scoring
- manager visibility
- repeat practice

If we skip these, we may still have an interesting demo, but not a real market-entry product.

## Bottom Line

To enter the market, we do not need to outbuild every competitor.

But we do need to cover the category’s core promise:

- let people practice real conversations
- measure how they did
- help them improve
- let organizations see readiness at scale

The most important practical checklist is:

- `scenario builder`
- `persona builder`
- `live simulation`
- `rubric scoring`
- `feedback report`
- `repeat practice`
- `manager dashboard`

That is the minimum credible stack.
