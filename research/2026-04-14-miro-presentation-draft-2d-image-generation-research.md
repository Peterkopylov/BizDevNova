# Presentation Draft: 2D Image Generation Market Research

## Slide 1. Title

`2D Image Generation Market Research`

Subtitle:

`Competitive landscape, market layers, and where Nova can play`

## Slide 2. Introduction

We focused specifically on the `2D image generation` space and adjacent categories.

The goal was not only to look at pure image generators, but also to understand the broader ecosystem around:

- creative tooling
- workflow layers
- business embedding
- adjacent 3D and control-based products

## Slide 3. What We Did

We analyzed and tested `45+ competitors`.

We looked at:

- product functionality
- pricing
- market positioning
- review signals
- funding and valuation signals
- workflow design
- control and consistency surfaces

This gave us a much clearer picture of how the market is structured and where competitive pressure is actually concentrated.

## Slide 4. What Is Clear Now

The market is not one flat category.

It is structured into `4 layers`.

These layers overlap, and many companies try to expand across them, but the distinction is still useful for understanding:

- product scope
- investment patterns
- strategic positioning

## Slide 5. Layer 1: Engine / Base Image Generation

This is the foundational layer:

- core generation models
- image quality
- speed
- model innovation

Representative model families:

- `FLUX` (`Black Forest Labs`)
- `Imagen` (`Google`)
- `GPT Image` (`OpenAI`)
- `Ideogram` (`Ideogram`)
- `Recraft` (`Recraft`)
- `Seedream` (`ByteDance / Seed`)

What matters here:

- model quality
- text rendering
- style control
- speed
- infrastructure scale

Key point:

At this layer it is more accurate to track `base models` than product wrappers, because many Layer 2 and Layer 3 companies build on top of these engines rather than competing here directly.

## Slide 6. Layer 2: Features on Top of Image Generation

This layer adds creative utility on top of generation:

- realtime interaction
- editing
- angle control
- relighting
- inpainting
- character tools
- LoRA / fine-tuning

Strong examples:

- `Krea` (`~$83M raised`, `~$500M valuation signal`)
- `Higgsfield` (`$130M total funding`, `>$1.3B valuation signal`)
- `OpenArt` (`~$35M raised`)
- `Leonardo AI` (`~$38.8M raised before Canva acquisition`)

Key observation:

This is the layer where many companies try to differentiate through feature power and interaction quality rather than through the base model alone.

## Slide 7. Layer 3: Workflow / Orchestration Layer

This layer is not just about generating images.

It is about building a repeatable creative system:

- multi-model workflows
- reusable pipelines
- structured creative canvases
- orchestration of tools and assets

Strong examples:

- `FLORA` (`$52M total funding`)
- `Figma Weave` (`Weavy raised $4M`, then acquired by Figma`)
- `Krea` (`~$83M raised`, `~$500M valuation signal`)
- `ComfyUI` (`~$16.2M funding signal`) as infrastructure-adjacent reference

Key observation:

This is the layer that currently appears most attractive from an investment perspective, because it sits above model commoditization and can become a system of work.

## Slide 8. Layer 4: Embedded Workflow / Business Solution

This layer integrates generation into a concrete business workflow.

Examples:

- ad creative production
- ecommerce visual workflows
- listing optimization
- vertical design tools

Strong examples:

- `Mintly`
- `Soona`
- `Ecomtent`
- `Rawshot`
- `Glorify`

Key observation:

These companies often monetize more directly because they sell business outcomes, not just creative capability.

## Slide 9. Everyone Is Expanding Across Layers

One of the clearest market signals:

Almost everyone is trying to move across multiple layers.

Examples:

- engine players add workflow and control
- feature players add orchestration
- workflow players move toward vertical solutions
- vertical solutions increasingly bundle more advanced generation and editing capabilities

This suggests that no single layer is fully defensible on its own for long.

## Slide 10. What This Means for Us

Right now, our current idea is closer to `one specific feature`.

That is interesting, but for `Layer 2` by itself it is probably not enough to support a strong standalone product.

It is a useful wedge, but too narrow to be the whole story.

## Slide 11. Strategic Direction

The more correct approach, also confirmed by Roman, is to use the feature as a wedge into `Nova`.

This can work as a `product-led, hype-led growth mechanism` for Nova.

In practice:

- use the feature as a strong attention-grabbing wedge
- bring users into `Nova`
- and then, inside Nova, shape the perception of a `Layer 2 product`

In other words:

- not just one AI trick
- but a broader set of AI features built on top of the `3D editor`
- and around the editor as a creative environment

## Slide 12. Product Framing Hypothesis

Potential framing:

`AI features on top of a 3D editor and around it`

That is strategically stronger than:

`one isolated image-generation feature`

Because it can combine:

- structure
- control
- scene editing
- consistency
- variation generation
- workflow expansion

## Slide 13. Why This Is Interesting

This gives us a more differentiated position:

- not just another image generator
- not just another wrapper around models
- but a tool where `3D structure becomes the control layer for AI generation`

This is much closer to a defendable product story.
