#### Two Broad Criteria for Promising AI Themes

Two broad directions seem especially promising when evaluating where a large and attractive AI-enabled business can emerge.

The first is markets where AI can change the traditional pricing model and better align incentives between provider and customer.

Why it matters:

- pricing innovation can unlock stronger customer adoption
- better incentive alignment can make the offer more compelling
- this creates room for a new model to outperform incumbents even without radically new technology

The second is automating work that is already being sent to offshore outsourcing.

Why it matters:

- demand is already proven
- the task is already structured enough to be delegated externally
- that often means it is more realistic to automate than work that remains deeply internal and undefined

The strongest case is when both conditions are true at the same time:

- AI can change pricing and incentive alignment
- the work is already outsourced and operationally defined

That overlap is not required, but when it exists it can be a particularly strong signal for market attractiveness.

#### Automation Platforms Need Process Discovery at the Core

One possible direction emerging from today's review is building platforms for business-process automation, or habit automation in the consumer case.

The key requirement is that the platform's core must be a strong system for discovering and formalizing the underlying process or habit before trying to automate or enforce it.

Why it matters:

- automation without process discovery tends to be too shallow for real practical use
- most users cannot clearly describe their workflows or habits upfront
- the real product value may come from helping users identify, structure, and operationalize repeatable patterns
- once a process is formalized, automation, monitoring, and optimization become much more credible

Strategic implication:

- the winning product may look less like a generic automation layer
- and more like a `process discovery -> formalization -> automation -> control` system

#### From Selling Software to Selling Problem Resolution

An emerging thesis is that `AI-native software` may itself become a short-lived positioning layer.

The stronger next step may be a shift from:

- `your business deserves software built to 2026 standards`

to something closer to:

- `your business deserves full problem-solving support delivered to 2027 standards`

The core idea is that software alone may soon become much less valuable as a standalone product.

Why:

- AI makes it easier to build software, so the software artifact itself becomes less differentiated
- if "almost anything" can be built, the harder and more valuable question becomes what should be built and how it should be used
- customers may care less about owning tools and more about getting outcomes

This suggests a shift from `software markets` toward `service markets powered by software`.

In that model, companies solve customer problems using whatever mix is needed:

- proprietary software
- AI systems and agents
- human experts
- operational workflows

Software becomes an internal or supporting tool rather than the thing being purchased on its own.

Potential implication:

- in many SMB and consumer categories, standalone software demand could weaken
- customers may instead buy packaged problem resolution
- software may increasingly be bundled, given away, or embedded inside the service layer

Strategic questions:

- Which customer problem are we actually solving?
- What must we provide besides software?
- How do we operationalize those additional services?
- How do we make the service delivery efficient and scalable?
- What internal software do we need to support the service?
- Which people do we need in addition to the software?
- What exactly do we charge for, and how should pricing work?

#### Ecosystems Make Products Easier to Grow

One of the most reliable ways to strengthen product distribution is to build an ecosystem around the product.

That ecosystem can include:

- developers
- partners
- distributors
- implementers
- service providers
- domain experts

The shared principle is simple: involve participants who can make money because the core product grows.

Why this helps distribution:

- ecosystem participants have their own incentive to promote the core product because its adoption increases their own income
- end users receive more value because they gain access not only to the core product, but also to complementary services and capabilities from the ecosystem

This creates a reinforcing loop where distribution and product value both improve at the same time.

One relevant pattern is an AI-agent company building an expert layer around its product, where users can get extra help from humans who complement the agent.

That can be done in two ways:

- a company can build its own ecosystem around a single AI agent
- or a platform can support ecosystems for many different AI-agent builders at once

Strategic implication:

- an AI agent may work not only as a product, but also as a demand-generation layer for paid human expertise
- this can turn the agent into the front door of a broader marketplace or service ecosystem

Useful questions:

- What types of experts could be gathered around this AI agent?
- Why would users of the AI agent want help from those experts?
- What services would those experts deliver?
- How would those services be delivered and priced?

Reverse framing:

- In which domains is there already a large supply of experts who want to monetize their expertise?
- What kind of AI agent could attract users in that domain?
- How could that agent convert enough users into paid expert services?
- Where could the platform take a share of expert revenue?

#### Win on a Visible Output, Then Expand into the Workspace

One GTM pattern appears to be:

- win on a highly visible business output
- then expand into the broader workspace where adjacent tasks also happen

Example framing:

- presentations are highly visible, high-stakes outputs
- success there can create trust, repetition, and internal visibility
- once the product is present in that workflow, it can expand into adjacent tasks in the same working environment

Why this matters:

- visible outputs are easier for users and teams to evaluate quickly
- they can create stronger word-of-mouth and internal proof than back-end workflow tools
- the initial wedge is concrete, but the expansion path can be broad

Strategic implication:

- the first product does not need to cover the whole workspace
- it may only need to dominate one output category that naturally connects to a larger set of tasks

#### Slow Creation Could Be a Feature, Not a Bug

An interesting inversion is that building something slowly inside an editor might itself be enjoyable.

Instead of positioning creation as pure speed and efficiency, the product could explore a mode where the experience feels more like a meditative 3D puzzle:

- tactile
- calm
- satisfying
- intentionally paced

Why this matters:

- not all creation products need to optimize for throughput
- some users may value immersion, focus, and emotional satisfaction over speed
- the act of assembling something piece by piece can itself become the entertainment or wellness value

Potential framing:

- `a meditative 3D building experience`
- `a creative 3D puzzle`
- `slow creation as a form of play`

Strategic implication:

- the editor experience could become a consumer product direction of its own
- this would be a different wedge from productivity, education, or professional creation

#### One Simple Idea with a Clear Target Can Be Enough

`Breakout Clips` is a useful reminder that a product does not always need a broad conceptual story to become legible.

A strong alternative is:

- one simple idea
- one clear target outcome
- one clear buyer/job

Observed pattern:

- the product promise is easy to understand in seconds
- the user does not need to learn a new category
- the target outcome is concrete: make social posts and ads that stop the scroll
- the product can still expand horizontally later, but the entry point is extremely narrow and legible

Why this matters:

- clarity lowers adoption friction
- a simple story is easier to advertise and demo
- users can immediately self-identify as relevant or not relevant
- strong positioning can come from a narrow, obvious use case rather than a broad platform pitch

Strategic takeaway:

- sometimes the best wedge is not a sophisticated category narrative
- it is a simple job with visible business value, packaged so clearly that the product explains itself
