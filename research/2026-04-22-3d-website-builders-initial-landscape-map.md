# 3D Website Builders: Initial Landscape Map

## Purpose

Start the new category `3D website builders` with a clean working map before creating individual competitor notes.

This note is meant to answer:

- what should count as a direct fit in this category
- which companies are strongest first profiling candidates
- where the boundary sits between `website builder`, `3D design tool`, and `AI interactive builder`

## Working Definition

For this project, `3D website builders` should mean:

- tools that help users create and publish websites
- where `3D` is a meaningful part of the product promise, not just a hidden advanced trick
- ideally with low-code or no-code workflows

This category should usually exclude:

- pure 3D asset tools with no real website-building workflow
- general website builders where 3D is possible but not a meaningful product wedge

## Short Take

This is not one flat category.

The market appears to split into at least four buckets:

- `native 3D-first website builders`
- `general website builders with AI and motion, but weaker 3D-native positioning`
- `3D design platforms that feed websites through embed/export workflows`
- `agentic interactive builders` that can generate websites and 3D experiences from natural language, but are broader than just websites

From the official positioning reviewed so far:

- `Dora` is the clearest direct fit
- `Spline` is strategically important, but looks more like a `3D experience platform` than a full website builder
- `Framer` and `Webflow` matter as adjacent major platforms, especially because they own real website publishing workflows and now have AI site-generation surfaces
- `Omma` looks like an important emerging wildcard because it combines `websites + 3D + natural language`, but it is broader than this category alone

## Why Each One Matters

### `Dora`

`Dora` is the strongest direct category anchor found in this pass.

Why:

- its homepage directly sells `3D animated websites`
- it combines `no-code website creation`, `3D objects/scenes`, and `AI site generation`
- it positions itself against workflow friction from tools like `Figma`, `Framer`, and `Webflow`

This makes Dora important as the most obvious answer to the category prompt:

- not just `can I add 3D to a site?`
- but `can I build and ship a 3D-first site without code?`

### `Spline`

`Spline` is highly relevant, but the product appears to sit one layer below a true site builder.

Why:

- Spline is explicitly a browser-based platform for `interactive production-ready experiences`
- it has dedicated `3D websites` positioning
- its docs show direct `Webflow` integration for no-code embedding and interaction control

So Spline matters because it may be:

- less of a standalone website builder
- more of the `3D engine / creation layer` inside the broader 3D web stack

That makes it strategically important even if it is not the cleanest direct competitor.

### `Framer`

`Framer` matters because it is a strong modern website builder with AI and design-led distribution.

But in this pass, its core positioning is:

- fast website creation
- AI-assisted layout generation
- responsive design, interactions, and publishing

It does not appear to position `native 3D website building` as centrally as Dora or Spline.

So Framer should likely be treated as:

- a major adjacent platform
- especially relevant if the category expands from `3D-native builder` into `design-led modern website builders with advanced motion`

### `Webflow`

`Webflow` matters for a similar reason to Framer, but with stronger `production site` and `team workflow` gravity.

Why:

- Webflow's AI site builder now turns prompts into `fully customized, production-ready` sites
- Webflow publicly teaches `3D` concepts and showcases a large community layer of 3D website examples
- Spline has an official Webflow integration, which suggests Webflow is an important downstream publishing surface for 3D experiences

So Webflow looks less like a pure 3D website builder and more like:

- a major website operating system
- that can absorb 3D through integrations, transforms, embeds, and community patterns

### `Omma`

`Omma` looks like the most interesting emerging wildcard in this category.

Why:

- it explicitly claims it can build `3D scenes, websites, games, apps`
- it frames the workflow as `Describe it. Omma builds it.`
- it sits at the intersection of `AI generation`, `interactive experience creation`, and `3D`

The reason to watch it closely is that it may represent a future category shift from:

- `website builder with some 3D`

to:

- `natural-language builder for interactive internet experiences`

For now, it is broader than the category, so it should be tracked as `emerging adjacent`, not as the only category definition.

## Initial Category Structure

The cleanest working structure so far is:

### 1. `Native 3D-first website builders`

Products where the website-builder promise and the 3D promise are both core.

Current strongest candidate:

- `Dora`

### 2. `3D web experience platforms`

Products that may not own the full website-building workflow, but are core tools for building the interactive 3D layer.

Current strongest candidate:

- `Spline`

### 3. `Modern website builders with AI + motion`

Products that own the publishable site workflow and may become strong competitors if 3D becomes a feature layer rather than a standalone category.

Current strongest candidates:

- `Framer`
- `Webflow`

### 4. `Agentic interactive builders`

Products that generate websites and 3D experiences from prompts, but sit in a broader creation category.

Current strongest candidate:

- `Omma`

## Recommended First Profiling Order

If we want to build this category the same way as the others, the best first competitor-note order appears to be:

1. `Dora`
2. `Spline`
3. `Framer`
4. `Webflow`
5. `Omma`

Reasoning:

- `Dora` is the clearest direct fit
- `Spline` may be the most strategically important adjacent layer
- `Framer` and `Webflow` are likely distribution and workflow gravity centers
- `Omma` may signal where the category is going next rather than where it is fully established today

## Practical Takeaway

The most important early insight is:

- `3D website builders` may look like a category on the surface
- but in practice it is probably a battle between `3D-native creation`, `website publishing workflows`, and `AI-generated interactive experiences`

That means we should avoid treating all of these companies as direct substitutes.

Instead, we should track:

- who owns `native 3D creation`
- who owns `real website publishing`
- who owns `AI-first prompt-to-site generation`

The winner may be the product that combines all three, not the one that is strongest in only one layer.
